WHAT IS HIP-HOP?

Click each section to learn more about the culture

Hip-hop emerged in the 1970s in the South Bronx, New York City. It was born from block parties where DJs would isolate and extend the percussion breaks of funk, soul, and disco songs. DJ Kool Herc is widely credited as the founding father, pioneering the "Merry-Go-Round" technique using two turntables to loop the break — a sound and approach that became the DNA of an entire culture.

Afrika Bambaataa codified hip-hop culture around four core elements: MCing (rapping), DJing, breakdancing (b-boying), and graffiti art. These four pillars work together to form the complete cultural expression — each influencing and amplifying the others into a unified movement that spread from the Bronx to every corner of the globe.

MCing began as the DJ's hype partner, keeping the crowd energised at block parties. It rapidly evolved into a sophisticated art form — storytelling, battle rhymes, social commentary, and lyrical poetry. From Coke La Rock to Rakim to the complex internal rhyme schemes that followed, the MC became the voice of a generation and the most recognisable face of hip-hop culture.

The DJ is the architect of hip-hop sound. Techniques like scratching, cutting, beat juggling, and the backspin were pioneered by Grandmaster Flash, DJ Grand Wizzard Theodore, and Afrika Bambaataa — transforming turntables from playback devices into musical instruments capable of producing entirely new sounds and keeping crowds in a perpetual state of motion.

B-boying emerged at DJ Kool Herc's parties — dancers responded physically to the break section of records. It evolved into a sophisticated athletic art form comprising top rock, footwork, freezes, and power moves. Crews like Rock Steady Crew and Dynamic Rockers turned b-boying into a competitive discipline that is now recognised as an Olympic sport.

Graffiti writers transformed the urban landscape into a canvas, developing a rich visual language from simple tags to elaborate wildstyle masterpieces. NYC subway cars became rolling galleries visible to millions. Writers like TAKI 183, Phase 2, Lee Quiñones, and Lady Pink elevated street writing into a globally recognised art movement — now displayed in galleries and museums worldwide.
